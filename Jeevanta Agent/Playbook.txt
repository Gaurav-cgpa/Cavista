RAG DOCUMENT 01 — SYMPTOM TO CONDITION MAPPING

Domain: Clinical Symptom Assessment
================================================================================

PURPOSE:
This document helps the AI agent map reported symptoms to likely clinical
conditions, generate intelligent follow-up questions, identify red flags,
and provide appropriate physician referral recommendations.

DISCLAIMER: All mappings are for clinical decision support only. Final
diagnosis must be made by a licensed medical professional.

================================================================================
PART 1: HEAD & NEUROLOGICAL SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: HEADACHE
─────────────────
Pattern 1 — Tension Headache
  Characteristics: Bilateral, band-like pressure, mild-moderate severity (3-6/10),
    no nausea/vomiting, no aura, worsens with stress/screen use/poor posture.
  Duration: 30 mins to 7 days.
  Triggers: Stress, poor sleep, dehydration, eye strain, skipped meals.
  Red flags: NONE typically.
  Follow-up questions:
    - "Is it on both sides of your head or just one?"
    - "Does it feel like a tight band or pressure?"
    - "Is it worse at the end of the workday?"
    - "Any neck stiffness or shoulder tension?"
  Likely condition: Tension-type headache (TTH)
  Recommendation: Stress management, posture correction, hydration, OTC analgesics.
  Refer to: GP; neurologist if chronic (>15 days/month).

Pattern 2 — Migraine
  Characteristics: Unilateral (often), throbbing/pulsating, moderate-severe (6-9/10),
    nausea/vomiting, photophobia, phonophobia; may have visual aura (flashing
    lights, zigzag lines) 20-30 mins before headache.
  Duration: 4 to 72 hours untreated.
  Triggers: Hormonal changes (menstruation), certain foods (aged cheese, red wine,
    chocolate, MSG, processed meats), bright lights, strong smells, sleep changes.
  Follow-up questions:
    - "Do you see flashing lights or blind spots before the headache?"
    - "Does light or sound bother you during the headache?"
    - "Do you feel nauseous or vomit?"
    - "Does it throb like a heartbeat?"
    - "Do you have to lie down in a dark room?"
  Likely condition: Migraine (with or without aura)
  Recommendation: Identify and avoid triggers, dark quiet room during attack.
  Refer to: Neurologist for prophylactic treatment if >4 attacks/month.

Pattern 3 — Cluster Headache
  Characteristics: Strictly unilateral, severe-excruciating (8-10/10), orbital/
    periorbital, associated with ipsilateral tearing, nasal congestion, red eye,
    restlessness. Short duration (15-180 mins) but multiple times/day in clusters
    lasting weeks.
  Likely condition: Cluster headache
  Refer to: Neurologist urgently.

Pattern 4 — EMERGENCY: Subarachnoid Hemorrhage
  Characteristics: "Thunderclap" — worst headache of life, sudden onset, reaching
    maximum severity in seconds. May have neck stiffness, vomiting, loss of
    consciousness.
  RED FLAG TRIGGER: "Is this the worst headache you've ever had and did it come
    on suddenly like a thunderclap?"
  Action: IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY — call 112. Do not wait.

Pattern 5 — Meningitis Headache
  Characteristics: Headache + fever + neck stiffness + photophobia. May have
    petechial rash (non-blanching spots — meningococcal).
  RED FLAG TRIGGER: Headache + fever + neck stiffness together.
  Action: IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY.

Pattern 6 — Hypertensive Headache
  Characteristics: Occipital (back of head), worse in morning, associated with
    high BP readings, visual changes possible.
  Follow-up: "Do you have high blood pressure? Have you checked your BP recently?"
  Refer to: GP urgently if BP >180/120.

Pattern 7 — Medication Overuse Headache
  Characteristics: Daily or near-daily headache in patient using pain medications
    >10-15 days/month. Headache improves briefly with medication but returns.
  Follow-up: "How often do you take pain medication for headaches?"
  Condition: Medication overuse headache (MOH / rebound headache)
  Refer to: Neurologist for withdrawal management.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: DIZZINESS / VERTIGO
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Differentiate first: Vertigo (room spinning) vs Lightheadedness (about to faint)
vs Disequilibrium (balance problem).

Pattern 1 — BPPV (Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo)
  Characteristics: Brief (seconds to 1-2 minutes) spinning sensation triggered
    by head position change (rolling over in bed, looking up, bending down).
    No hearing loss, no tinnitus.
  Likely condition: BPPV — most common cause of vertigo
  Refer to: ENT or physiotherapist (Epley manoeuvre is curative).

Pattern 2 — Vestibular Neuritis / Labyrinthitis
  Characteristics: Sudden severe vertigo lasting days, worsened by movement.
    May have hearing loss (labyrinthitis). Often preceded by viral illness.
  Likely condition: Vestibular neuritis / labyrinthitis
  Refer to: ENT.

Pattern 3 — Ménière's Disease
  Characteristics: Episodic vertigo lasting 20 mins to hours + unilateral
    hearing loss + tinnitus (ringing) + aural fullness (ear pressure).
  Likely condition: Ménière's disease
  Refer to: ENT specialist.

Pattern 4 — Orthostatic Hypotension
  Characteristics: Lightheadedness/near-fainting upon standing up quickly.
    Resolves in seconds-minutes. Common in elderly, dehydrated, or those on
    antihypertensives.
  Follow-up: "Does it happen specifically when you stand up from sitting or
    lying down?"
  Likely condition: Orthostatic hypotension
  Refer to: GP; review medications.

Pattern 5 — EMERGENCY: Central Vertigo (Stroke/TIA)
  Characteristics: Sudden onset, persistent. Associated with double vision,
    facial droop, limb weakness, slurred speech, difficulty walking.
  RED FLAG TRIGGER: Any neurological symptoms with vertigo.
  Action: IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY — call 112.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: FAINTING / SYNCOPE
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Vasovagal syncope: triggered by standing, pain, fear, heat. Brief loss of
    consciousness with warning (nausea, sweating, tunnel vision). Benign.
  Cardiac syncope: No warning. Occurs during exercise. Associated with
    palpitations or chest pain. REFER URGENTLY to cardiologist.
  RED FLAG: Syncope during exertion or associated with chest pain/palpitations.

PART 2: CHEST SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: CHEST PAIN
───────────────────
Pattern 1 — EMERGENCY: Acute Coronary Syndrome (Heart Attack / Unstable Angina)
  Characteristics: Central/left-sided crushing, pressure, or tightness.
    Radiation to left arm, jaw, neck, or shoulder. Associated diaphoresis
    (sweating), nausea, vomiting, dyspnea. May present atypically in women
    and diabetics (fatigue, epigastric pain, jaw pain only).
  Duration: >20 minutes (AMI), relieved by GTN (angina).
  RED FLAG TRIGGER: Chest pain + radiation + sweating/nausea.
  Action: IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY — call 112. Chew aspirin 325mg if available
    and no allergy.
  Refer to: Emergency department immediately.

Pattern 2 — Stable Angina
  Characteristics: Exertional chest tightness/pressure, relieved by rest or
    GTN within 5 minutes. Predictable, reproducible.
  Follow-up: "Does the chest pain come on when you exercise or climb stairs?
    Does it go away when you rest?"
  Likely condition: Stable angina pectoris
  Refer to: Cardiologist — stress test, angiography may be needed.

Pattern 3 — EMERGENCY: Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
  Characteristics: Sudden-onset pleuritic chest pain (sharp, worse with
    breathing), dyspnea, tachycardia, may have haemoptysis. Risk factors:
    recent immobility/surgery/long-haul flight, DVT, oral contraceptive use,
    cancer.
  RED FLAG TRIGGER: Sudden chest pain + breathlessness + recent immobility.
  Action: IMMEDIATE EMERGENCY.

Pattern 4 — GERD / Acid Reflux (Oesophageal)
  Characteristics: Burning retrosternal pain, worse after meals, lying down,
    spicy/fatty food. Associated with regurgitation, sour taste, belching.
    Relieved by antacids.
  Follow-up: "Is it a burning sensation? Is it worse after eating or when
    you lie down?"
  Likely condition: GERD / gastro-oesophageal reflux disease
  Refer to: GP; gastroenterologist if persistent.

Pattern 5 — Musculoskeletal Chest Pain (Costochondritis)
  Characteristics: Localized, sharp, reproducible with palpation or movement.
    Worse with deep breathing or pressing on chest wall.
  Follow-up: "Does it hurt more when I press on your chest? Or when you twist
    or take a deep breath?"
  Likely condition: Costochondritis / musculoskeletal pain
  Refer to: GP; NSAIDs usually effective.

Pattern 6 — Anxiety / Panic Attack
  Characteristics: Chest tightness/pain with palpitations, shortness of breath,
    tingling in fingers/lips, dizziness, feeling of impending doom. Peaks
    within 10 minutes. Associated with high anxiety or panic disorder.
  Follow-up: "Do you feel very anxious or like something terrible is about to
    happen during these episodes?"
  Likely condition: Panic attack / anxiety disorder
  Refer to: GP; psychiatrist or psychologist.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: PALPITATIONS
─────────────────────
  Benign causes: Caffeine, stress, anxiety, dehydration, anaemia, thyroid.
  Concerning features: Palpitations + syncope, palpitations at rest, irregular
    rhythm, chest pain, dyspnea.
  Follow-up: "Do they happen at rest or with activity? Any dizziness or
    fainting? Do they start and stop suddenly?"
  RED FLAG: Palpitations + syncope → Refer to cardiologist urgently (ECG needed).
  Likely conditions: SVT, atrial fibrillation, ectopic beats, anxiety.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: SHORTNESS OF BREATH (DYSPNEA)
───────────────────────────────────────
  Acute onset + wheeze: Asthma exacerbation or anaphylaxis.
  Acute onset + pleuritic pain + tachycardia: Pulmonary embolism (EMERGENCY).
  Gradual + exertional + ankle swelling + orthopnea: Heart failure.
  Chronic + productive cough + smoking history: COPD.
  Exertional + young patient + wheeze: Asthma.
  At rest in anxious patient: Hyperventilation / panic disorder.
  Follow-up: "Can you lie flat at night? Do your ankles swell? Any cough or
    wheezing? Did it come on suddenly or gradually?"
  RED FLAG: Sudden severe dyspnea at rest → EMERGENCY.

PART 3: ABDOMINAL SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: ABDOMINAL PAIN
────────────────────────
Pattern 1 — GERD / Peptic Ulcer Disease
  Location: Epigastric (upper central abdomen).
  GERD: Burning, worse after meals, lying down. Relieved by antacids.
  PUD: Epigastric gnawing/burning pain. Duodenal ulcer: relieved by food.
    Gastric ulcer: worsened by food. H. pylori is common cause.
  Follow-up: "Is it a burning pain in the upper stomach area? Is it better or
    worse after eating?"
  Refer to: GP; endoscopy if persistent or alarm features.

Pattern 2 — Appendicitis
  Location: Starts periumbilical, migrates to right iliac fossa (RIF).
  Characteristics: Continuous, worsening pain. Fever, nausea, vomiting.
    Rebound tenderness (McBurney's point). Worse with movement.
  RED FLAG TRIGGER: RIF pain + fever + worsening over hours → EMERGENCY.
  Action: Emergency surgical evaluation immediately.

Pattern 3 — Gallbladder Disease (Cholecystitis / Gallstones)
  Location: Right upper quadrant (RUQ), may radiate to right shoulder/back.
  Triggers: Fatty meals, large meals.
  Characteristics: Colicky or constant RUQ pain, nausea, vomiting.
    Fever if infected (cholecystitis).
  Follow-up: "Is the pain in the upper right side? Is it worse after fatty
    meals? Any yellowing of the skin or eyes?"
  Refer to: GP/surgeon; ultrasound abdomen.

Pattern 4 — Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
  Characteristics: Crampy lower abdominal pain, relieved by defecation.
    Associated with bloating, altered bowel habits (diarrhea OR constipation
    OR alternating). No blood, no fever, no weight loss.
  Triggers: Stress, certain foods (FODMAPs: wheat, dairy, onion, garlic).
  Follow-up: "Is the pain relieved when you pass stool? Any bloating? Does
    stress make it worse?"
  Likely condition: IBS
  Refer to: GP; dietitian for low-FODMAP diet.

Pattern 5 — EMERGENCY: Bowel Obstruction
  Characteristics: Colicky diffuse pain, absolute constipation (no flatus),
    distension, vomiting (bilious or feculent).
  RED FLAG: No bowel movement + vomiting + distension → EMERGENCY.

Pattern 6 — Urinary Tract Infection (UTI) / Pyelonephritis
  Location: Suprapubic (bladder) or loin/flank (kidney).
  UTI: Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain, cloudy/smelly urine.
  Pyelonephritis: Fever, rigors, loin pain (CVA tenderness), nausea.
  Follow-up: "Any pain or burning when you urinate? Needing to go urgently
    or frequently? Any fever or back pain?"
  Refer to: GP; urine culture; urgent if febrile.

Pattern 7 — Kidney Stones (Renal Colic)
  Location: Loin to groin — severe colicky, radiation to groin/testicle/labia.
  Characteristics: One of the most severe pains. Restless patient (cannot
    get comfortable). Haematuria (blood in urine) in 90%.
  Follow-up: "Does the pain come in waves? Does it move from your back towards
    your groin? Any blood in the urine?"
  Refer to: GP/urologist urgently; CT KUB.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: NAUSEA & VOMITING
──────────────────────────
  Acute + diarrhea + fever: Gastroenteritis (viral or food poisoning).
  With headache + photophobia: Migraine or meningitis.
  With jaundice + RUQ pain: Liver/gallbladder disease.
  Persistent AM nausea in female of reproductive age: Pregnancy (rule out).
  With medications: Drug-induced (metformin, opioids, antibiotics common).
  Vomiting blood (haematemesis): EMERGENCY → GI bleed.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: DIARRHEA
─────────────────
  Acute (<2 weeks): Likely infectious (viral gastroenteritis, food poisoning,
    traveler's diarrhea). Manage with hydration (ORS).
  Bloody diarrhea: Bacterial (Salmonella, Shigella, E. coli, Campylobacter),
    IBD, ischemic colitis. Refer urgently.
  Chronic (>4 weeks): IBD (Crohn's, ulcerative colitis), IBS, malabsorption
    (coeliac disease), thyroid disease. Refer to gastroenterologist.
  RED FLAG: Blood in stool + weight loss + age >50 → colonoscopy urgently.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: CONSTIPATION
─────────────────────
  Functional: Diet low in fibre and water, sedentary lifestyle, IBS.
  Drug-induced: Opioids, iron supplements, antacids (calcium/aluminium),
    antidepressants (TCAs), antihypertensives (calcium channel blockers).
  Hypothyroidism: Associated with fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance.
  RED FLAG: New constipation in >50 + blood/mucus/weight loss → colonoscopy.

PART 4: MUSCULOSKELETAL SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: JOINT PAIN (ARTHRALGIA / ARTHRITIS)
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Pattern 1 — Osteoarthritis (OA)
  Joints: Weight-bearing (knees, hips), hands (DIPs), spine.
  Characteristics: Pain worsens with activity, improves with rest. Morning
    stiffness <30 minutes. Bony crepitus. No systemic features.
  Age: Usually >50, associated with obesity.
  Refer to: GP; physiotherapy; orthopaedic surgeon for severe cases.

Pattern 2 — Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
  Joints: Symmetrical small joints (MCPs, PIPs, wrists), spares DIPs.
  Characteristics: Morning stiffness >1 hour, improves with movement.
    Systemic features: fatigue, low-grade fever, weight loss.
    May have subcutaneous nodules.
  Follow-up: "Is the stiffness worst in the morning and does it loosen up
    as the day goes on? Is it in the same joints on both sides of your body?"
  Refer to: Rheumatologist. Early treatment prevents joint destruction.

Pattern 3 — Gout
  Joints: Monoarthritis, classically first MTP joint (big toe), ankle, knee.
  Characteristics: Sudden onset of severe pain, swelling, redness, warmth.
    Often at night. Triggered by alcohol, red meat, seafood, dehydration.
    Patients cannot bear weight.
  Follow-up: "Did the pain come on suddenly, especially overnight? Is the
    joint red and hot? Any recent alcohol or rich food?"
  Refer to: GP; serum uric acid; colchicine/NSAIDs acutely; allopurinol for
    prevention.

Pattern 4 — Septic Arthritis
  Characteristics: Single joint — hot, red, swollen, extremely tender. Fever,
    unable to move joint. EMERGENCY — joint destruction within 24-48 hours.
  RED FLAG: Single hot swollen joint + fever → EMERGENCY orthopaedic review.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: LOW BACK PAIN
──────────────────────
  Mechanical (90%): Worsened with movement, relieved with rest. No neurological
    features. Related to posture, muscle strain, disc disease.
  Sciatica: Pain radiating down leg in L4/L5/S1 dermatomal distribution.
    Numbness/tingling in leg. Positive straight leg raise.
  RED FLAG (Cauda Equina Syndrome): Saddle anaesthesia (numbness around
    anus/perineum) + bladder/bowel dysfunction + bilateral leg weakness.
    Action: EMERGENCY neurosurgical referral.
  Cancer/Infection: Night pain, age >50, history of cancer, weight loss,
    fever, IV drug use — refer urgently for imaging.

PART 5: RESPIRATORY SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: COUGH
──────────────
  Acute (<3 weeks): URTI (viral), pneumonia (fever + productive cough +
    dyspnea), COVID-19.
  Subacute (3-8 weeks): Post-infectious, pertussis (whooping cough).
  Chronic (>8 weeks):
    - Dry cough: ACE inhibitor side effect, GERD, asthma (cough variant).
    - Productive: COPD, bronchiectasis, TB.
    - Haemoptysis (coughing blood): TB, lung cancer, PE → REFER URGENTLY.
  Follow-up: "Is it dry or are you bringing up phlegm? What colour is it?
    Any blood? How long have you had it?"
  RED FLAG: Blood in sputum (haemoptysis) → urgent chest X-ray + referral.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: FEVER
──────────────
  With localising signs: Treat based on source (UTI → antibiotics, etc.)
  Without localising signs:
    - Tropical context: Malaria (India — common), dengue, typhoid, leptospirosis.
    - Dengue features: High fever (>39°C), severe headache, retro-orbital pain,
      rash, myalgia, thrombocytopenia.
    - Typhoid: Stepwise fever, rose spots, relative bradycardia, splenomegaly.
    - Malaria: Cyclical fevers (tertian/quartan), rigors, sweating, headache.
  Follow-up: "Have you traveled to rural areas or forests recently? Any
    mosquito exposure? Any rash with the fever?"
  Refer to: GP; blood tests (CBC, malarial parasite, NS1 antigen for dengue,
    Widal test for typhoid).

PART 6: ENDOCRINE & METABOLIC SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: FATIGUE / TIREDNESS
─────────────────────────────
  Hypothyroidism: Fatigue + weight gain + constipation + cold intolerance +
    dry skin + bradycardia + depression. TSH elevated.
  Hyperthyroidism: Fatigue + weight loss + palpitations + heat intolerance +
    tremor + diarrhea + anxiety. TSH suppressed.
  Diabetes: Fatigue + polyuria + polydipsia + weight loss. FBS elevated.
  Anaemia: Fatigue + pallor + dyspnea on exertion + palpitations. Hb low.
  Depression: Fatigue + low mood + anhedonia + sleep disturbance.
  Chronic Fatigue Syndrome: Persistent fatigue >6 months, not relieved by
    rest, post-exertional malaise.
  Sleep apnea: Non-restorative sleep + snoring + daytime somnolence.
  Follow-up: "Do you feel tired even after a full night's sleep? Any weight
    changes? Do you feel cold more than usual? Any low mood?"

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: UNEXPLAINED WEIGHT LOSS
─────────────────────────────────
  Definition: >5% body weight over 6-12 months without intentional dieting.
  With increased appetite: Hyperthyroidism, uncontrolled diabetes, malabsorption.
  With decreased appetite: Malignancy, depression, chronic infection (TB, HIV),
    chronic renal/liver failure.
  RED FLAG: Any unexplained significant weight loss → full workup urgently.

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SYMPTOM: EXCESSIVE THIRST (POLYDIPSIA) & FREQUENT URINATION (POLYURIA)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Classic triad of diabetes: Polyuria + polydipsia + weight loss.
  Diabetes insipidus: Extreme polyuria with very dilute urine (rare).
  Hypercalcaemia: Polyuria + constipation + bone pain + psychiatric symptoms.
  Follow-up: "Are you drinking much more water than usual? Do you urinate
    very frequently, including waking at night to urinate?"
  Refer to: GP; fasting blood glucose + HbA1c.

PART 7: SKIN SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: RASH
─────────────
  With fever + non-blanching: Meningococcal septicaemia → EMERGENCY.
  With fever + target lesion: Lyme disease (if tick exposure).
  With fever + maculopapular: Dengue, drug reaction, viral exanthem.
  Vesicular (blisters): Herpes simplex, herpes zoster (shingles — dermatomal).
  Itchy, urticarial (hives): Allergic reaction — check for anaphylaxis signs.
  Butterfly facial rash: Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) — + arthritis +
    fatigue + photosensitivity.
  Scaly plaques (elbows/knees/scalp): Psoriasis.
  Follow-up: "Is the rash itchy, painful, or neither? Does it blanch when
    you press on it? Any fever accompanying it?"
  RED FLAG: Non-blanching rash + fever → EMERGENCY.

PART 8: MENTAL HEALTH SYMPTOMS
================================================================================

SYMPTOM: LOW MOOD / DEPRESSION
────────────────────────────────
  Core features (PHQ-9 basis):
    - Depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day
    - Loss of interest/pleasure (anhedonia) in previously enjoyed activities
    - Sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia)
    - Fatigue and loss of energy
    - Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
    - Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
    - Psychomotor agitation or retardation
    - Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation
  Duration: At least 2 weeks for MDD diagnosis.
  Severity: Mild (PHQ ≤9), Moderate (10-19), Severe (≥20).
  RED FLAG: Any suicidal ideation → provide crisis resources immediately.
    India crisis lines: iCall: 9152987821 | Vandrevala: 1860-2662-345
    | NIMHANS: 080-46110007
  Refer to: GP for mild; psychiatrist for moderate-severe; URGENT for suicidal.

SYMPTOM: ANXIETY
────────────────
  GAD: Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple areas of life, most
    days, for >6 months. Associated with muscle tension, fatigue, irritability,
    poor concentration, sleep disturbance.
  Panic disorder: Recurrent unexpected panic attacks. Fear of future attacks.
    Avoidance behaviour.
  Social anxiety: Fear of social situations, fear of embarrassment/judgment.
  Follow-up: "Do you find yourself worrying a lot even when there's no
    specific reason? Does your heart race or do you feel short of breath
    during anxious moments?"
  Refer to: GP; psychologist for CBT; psychiatrist if severe or not improving.

SYMPTOM: SLEEP DISTURBANCE (INSOMNIA)
───────────────────────────────────────
  Primary insomnia: Difficulty initiating or maintaining sleep without
    identifiable cause.
  Secondary: Anxiety, depression, pain, sleep apnea, medications (steroids,
    beta-blockers, stimulants), alcohol, caffeine.
  Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA): Loud snoring + witnessed apnoeas + excessive
    daytime sleepiness + morning headaches. Risk factors: obesity, large neck,
    male sex. Refer for polysomnography.

================================================================================

Headache + fever + neck stiffness        | Meningitis               | Call 112 NOW
Sudden weakness/speech/facial droop      | Stroke                   | Call 112 NOW
Non-blanching rash + fever               | Meningococcal sepsis     | Call 112 NOW
